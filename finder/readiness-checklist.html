Check you are grant ready

Last Updated: 02 March 2021

Whether you’re looking to apply for a specific grant or preparing for the next exciting grant opportunity there are things you should know and do before you decide to proceed. Being more aware will help reduce the risk of wasted time, money and effort on an unsuccessful application.
Open all

1 I understand the grant process.

The process of getting a grant can vary. Generally, you will need to:

  • meet the eligibility requirement for the grant
  • invest time to write your application
  • report on how you will spend the money.

For some grants, you will be required to match the funding amount with your own money.

Essential things to determine upfront is:

  • Whether the grant is competitive – Competitive grants have limited fundingthat is awarded based on the merit of the application.
  • How it will be assessed – Do you need to answer some simple questions, or is there weighted criteria or objectives that your application needs to align to?
  • The submission process – Get to know the online grant application system so you can allow enough time for populating and submitting your application, including any mandatory attachments.

2 I have read and understood the grant guidelines

To avoid disappointment, ensure you read the grant guidelines and understand them before deciding toapply. It contains important information on your eligibility and the grant assessment criteria.

When reading the grant guidelines make sure:

  • You have the right organisational status
  • Your project/program meets the funding body’s priorities for that round. Priorities can change from one round to the next, so don’t assume that just because your project was eligible last time, it’s going to be eligible again.
  • You are based in the right geographic area
  • Whether partners are required
  • That you can deliver the project/program in the required time
  • Whether there is a co-contribution required (either cash or in-kind)
  • That you can manage and acquit the grant funds you are applying for
  • That you meet both the applicant criteria and the project/program criteria. These two elements are different, so check both sections of the guidelines.

If, after reading the guidelines, you are still unsure of youreligibility or have questions email or call the contacts provided on the grant page.

3 I understand the time commitment of the grant.

The time to compile, write and submit an application can vary a lot based on the grant. This will take time away from other important business activities. Determine the amount of time it will take to:

  • Retrieve or compile business information and supporting evidence required in the application
  • Write the application
  • Review application
  • Populate the application in the grant portal and trouble shooting.

Be realistic on whether this is achievable alongside your current commitments.It may also be beneficial to also do a cost/benefit/ and risk analysis for pursuing the grant to help you decide.

4 I am proactive and committed to the process.

Successful applications take time and effort to get right.Ask yourself whether, in addition to your current workload, if you have the time to source, compile, write, review and submit a clear and compelling application by its deadline?

If the answer is 'no', consider creating a team of capable people within your business to write, manage and submit the application.

Beready for future opportunities. Move away from reactive grant seeking and incorporate grants into your ongoing business or financial strategy. This could include mapping out an annual calendar of grant applications and reviewing your eligibility status for these programs.

6 I have the required business information.

Grants or programs will generally have criteria your business must meet to be eligible. To determine your eligibility, you may be asked to provide information about:

  • your business's annual turnover
  • how long you've been running your business
  • what problem you're trying to solve
  • why you need funding
  • your customers or market
  • how your product or service can help to meet a specific need.

Grant applications are much easier to write if you have the documentation readily available. Keep in mind each grant application will have unique criteria that needs to be addressed. Don’t be tempted to cut and paste information that doesn’t fit.

If you don't have the documentation, what steps are needed to create them? Ensure you factor this time into your grant application timeline.

Business.gov.au has valuable resources and templates to help you create common business documents including:

  • How to create a business plan
  • How to create a marketing plan
  • How to create a budget
  • Develop a new product
  • How to develop an innovation strategy
  • Intellectual property explained

Other useful documents to keep on hand for future grant opportunities include:

  • Useful statistics (for your own school/business/organisation, region and state)
  • Project overviews
  • Project costings
  • Statement of need (why the project is needed)
  • Letters of support (don't forget to ask your local MP and other schools/businesses/organisations who support your idea).

7 I have a clear objective that aligns with the grant. 

Grants are a way for government to enact their policy. They usually fund aspecific project of a business rather than the whole organisation. Think about your funding needs in terms of a project that can be clearly defined and costedand demonstrate how your project aligns with the grant objectives.

Convince the grant assessors of your claims against the evaluation / assessment / selection criteria – Some ways to do thiscould be to include:

  • Customer surveys/letters of support
  • Market research / growth forecasts
  • Feasibility studies
  • Forward projections
  • Competitor analysis
  • Abilities of key staff (e.g. resumes)
  • Other project examples
  • Risk Analysis (larger projects)
  • Link to Government policies/agenda (e.g.Jobs) - remember your audience!

8 I can write an application that is clear, concise and compelling.

Having a certain amount of skill in writing is beneficial when it comes to writing grant applications. You will need to describe your organisation and activities in a clear, comprehensive and consistent manner, and make a convincing case for why you should receive funding.

When applying for grants in general, make sure you:

  • address the merit criteria
  • show you've done your research
  • use evidence to back up your claims and reference your data.

Make it clear

For competitive grants, make your submission is easy to read so the assessor can see:

  • What you will do
  • How it connects with the government's objectives
  • Your ability to do it
  • How much money you need and what it will pay for
  • What the expected results will be at the end of the project

Write then revise, followed by write then revise. You might also consider using a 'Plain English' guide; such as www.26ten.tas.gov.au

Make it concise

Ensure your application:

  • Isn't repetitive: Each criterion seeks different information - don't repeat the same thing
  • Adds quality information - rather than quantity. Assessors read many applications, so if yours has unnecessary detail, it makes it harder for them to assess
  • Don't be wordy or vague (e.g., write' because'…. not …. 'owing to the fact that')

Make it compelling

Make sure your information:

  • Is accurate and factual – both content and the figures
  • Includes credible data to back up your proposal
  • Shows you consulted key people & represented their views.
  • Presents a strong case with clear links between the project, your firm & the grant objectives
  • Includes all evidence and financial information requested
  • Explains how you plan to meet the grant program's 'Key Performance Indicators' (KPIs). Your KPIs must be realistic, achievable and based on facts… not exaggerated or inflated.

If you aren’t confident in your writing abilities allow more time in your grants timeline and seek help from someone you trust with proof reading. Alternatively, you maychoose to outsource the grants application writing and managementto a professional grant's writer.

9 I’ve learnt from my unsuccessful applications

If you weren't successful in your last grant application, don't be discouraged. See it as a learning opportunity.

  • Seek feedback: Most grant programs encourage you to seek feedback about why your application wasn't successful. Take every opportunity you can to see how you can improve future applications.
  • Review your application: Make notes on your application about the aspects you can improve on next time.
  • Be proactive: Keep your project/business plan up-to-date so you are ready to act quickly as opportunities arise.
